### Context

The Union Cabinet approved the National Investment Policy for Urea-2026 (NIPU-2026) on Wednesday as part of the Atmanirbhar Bharat initiative to achieve self-reliance in urea production. The policy aims to incentivize domestic manufacturing and reduce the country's dependence on imports for this critical fertilizer input.

### The debate

**The Ministry:** The Cabinet's approval of NIPU-2026 operationalises the Atmanirbhar Bharat mandate by targeting self-reliance in urea production, a sector where India currently imports nearly a third of its annual requirement (pib). The policy is designed to insulate farmers from global price volatility and supply chain disruptions by incentivising domestic capacity additions and reviving stranded assets. By securing the feedstock and investment framework for indigenous manufacturing, the Ministry is addressing the structural import dependency that has persisted for decades (indian_express).

**The Opposition:** The Ministry frames NIPU-2026 as a structural fix for import dependency, yet the PIB release offers no concrete import-reduction targets, no timeline for achieving self-reliance, and no committed budget for the "incentivising" it claims. (pib) Without those specifics, the policy is a branding exercise, not a delivery mechanism. The government has announced urea self-reliance targets before — in 2017, 2019, and 2021 — each time citing new investment policies, and each time imports have risen rather than fallen. (indian_express) A genuine Atmanirbhar push would fix the broken gas-pricing formula that makes domestic urea uncompetitive against imported product, and it would commit to a fixed import-quota glide path; NIPU-2026 does neither.

**The Ministry (rebuttal):** The Opposition correctly notes that the PIB release does not publish a year-by-year import-quota glide path or a new gas-pricing formula, but it misreads the policy's architecture: NIPU-2026 is an investment-framework notification, not a fiscal-budget document or a gas-pricing order, and those levers sit with the Ministry of Petroleum and the Cabinet Committee on Economic Affairs respectively (pib). The charge that past targets failed because imports rose ignores the structural shift already underway — three brownfield plants at Gorakhpur, Sindri and Barauni have been revived and the Talcher coal-gasification unit commissioned since 2019, adding roughly 6.3 million tonnes of indigenous capacity that did not exist in the 2017 baseline (indian_express). By extending the investment window and de-risking feedstock linkages for new greenfield and brownfield projects, NIPU-2026 locks in the next capacity tranche rather than recycling old slogans.

**The Opposition (closing):** The Ministry's rebuttal confirms the core problem: it treats the revival of plants announced years ago as proof that *this* policy works, while admitting NIPU-2026 itself contains no import-reduction target, no gas-price fix, and no committed budget. [internal contradiction] They cite 6.3 million tonnes of capacity added since 2019 as a structural shift, yet imports have risen in that same period — proving capacity alone does not displace imports without the pricing and offtake guarantees the policy omits. Extending an "investment window" without de-risking the feedstock cost or mandating an import glide path is not locking in the next tranche; it is recycling the same hope that failed in 2017, 2019, and 2021. A closing document that delegates every hard lever to another ministry while claiming the Atmanirbhar mantle is not a delivery mechanism — it is a press release masquerading as policy.
